Case Study 01 — Volume

Combine Design Bangalore & Hyderabad

How a South India studio filled a high-volume sales pipeline with 400 leads a month and turned it into ₹1CR/M — without relying on referrals or builder contacts.

The situation

Combine Design runs out of two of South India's most competitive interior design markets at once — Bangalore and Hyderabad. Before this system was built, the studio's growth was capped by the same thing that caps most design businesses in these cities: dependence on referrals, builder tie-ups, and word-of-mouth. That pipeline is real, but it's slow, unpredictable, and it doesn't scale on command. Combine Design didn't have a demand problem with the market — they had a supply problem with leads. They had a sales team big enough and sharp enough to handle far more conversations than referrals alone could ever generate, and that team was sitting under-utilised.

The brief was straightforward: build a lead engine that could feed a high-capacity sales team every single day, in two cities at once, without the studio having to lift a finger on outreach.

Why volume was the right call here

Two things made a volume-first model the obvious choice for Combine Design rather than a filtration model. First, the sales team was already structured for high call volume — multiple sales reps trained to qualify over the phone, not just close warm leads. Second, Bangalore and Hyderabad are large enough markets that demand for home interiors doesn't run out; the ceiling on growth was lead supply, not market size. Given that combination, the highest-leverage move wasn't to spend budget engineering a smaller, more filtered funnel — it was to remove as much friction as possible from the top of the funnel and let a strong sales process do the qualifying that the ad platform otherwise would have.

The approach: volume, not filtration

  • Meta Instant Forms as the primary capture mechanism. Instead of sending traffic to a landing page, ads open a native lead form directly inside Facebook and Instagram. Because the form pre-fills with the user's existing details, submitting takes seconds, not minutes — and that drop in friction is what unlocks lead volume at this scale. A website-first funnel simply could not have produced 400 leads a month at a sustainable cost per lead in these markets.
  • Broad but structured targeting. "Volume" didn't mean throwing the net wide and hoping. Targeting was layered across both cities by budget indicators, home-ownership and moving-in signals, and renovation-stage behaviour (people actively in the "planning" window versus people just browsing inspiration). This kept the lead pool large without letting it collapse into pure junk.
  • Creative testing at scale. With this much ad spend flowing through the account, multiple creative variants — different hooks, formats, and offers — were run simultaneously and rotated based on performance. This is what kept cost-per-lead stable as volume climbed, instead of the account fatiguing and CPLs creeping up month over month.
  • Two-city campaign architecture. Bangalore and Hyderabad were run as distinct campaigns with their own budgets, creative angles, and targeting logic rather than one blended campaign — because a message and price point that resonates in one city doesn't automatically translate to the other.
  • Fast-follow sales process built around volume. With this many leads landing every day, the entire sales motion was built around speed-to-lead: the faster a lead is called after submitting the form, the higher the chance of a real conversation. Qualification happens on the call, not on the form — the sales team's job is to sort the 400 into "real, budget-fit, ready to move" versus "not yet," rather than relying on the ad platform to pre-sort them.

Campaign structure & ad split

Two campaign types run at all times, per city, rather than one blended account:

  • Prospecting campaigns (roughly 65–70% of budget). Objective set to on-platform leads, targeting cold audiences via interest stacking (home décor, real estate, new-home-buyer behaviour), lookalike audiences built from the studio's highest-value past clients, and geo-radius targeting around newly-handed-over residential projects and large apartment complexes in both cities — where renovation intent is naturally highest right after possession.
  • Retargeting campaigns (roughly 20–25% of budget). Served to website visitors, video viewers past the 50% mark, and Instant Form openers who didn't complete — a warmer pool that's cheaper to convert and pulls extra volume out of traffic that would otherwise be wasted.
  • Creative-testing budget (roughly 10% of budget). A standing slice ring-fenced purely for testing new ad angles and formats before they're rolled into the main prospecting campaigns, so testing never disrupts the performance of what's already working.

Budget between the two cities isn't fixed — it's rebalanced weekly based on cost-per-lead and lead-to-call-conversion, typically landing close to an even split but shifting toward whichever city is producing cheaper, better-qualified volume in a given week.

Ad formats & creative mix

  • Before/after transformation reels — short-form video showing real project transformations, which consistently pulls the lowest cost-per-lead of any format in the account.
  • Carousel ads showcasing multiple completed rooms or full-home projects, aimed at users further along in their research who want to see range and consistency of work.
  • UGC-style static and testimonial ads framed as a client talking about their experience, used to build trust quickly with cold audiences who've never heard of the studio.
  • New creative variants are shipped weekly and rotated in and out based on frequency and CPL, so the account never runs on stale creative long enough to fatigue.

The funnel, step by step

  1. Ad served on Facebook and Instagram feed, Stories, and Reels placements to the layered cold or retargeting audience above.
  2. Instant Form opens in-platform the moment someone taps the ad — pre-filled with their name and number, plus 2–3 short qualifying questions (renovation budget range, city, and timeline) so the sales team has context before the first call.
  3. Lead lands in CRM instantly and triggers an automated WhatsApp/SMS confirmation to the lead, keeping them warm in the seconds before a rep calls.
  4. Sales rep calls within minutes, not hours — speed-to-lead is treated as a hard KPI, because at this volume the leads that get called fastest close at a meaningfully higher rate.
  5. Qualification happens live on the call, where the rep confirms budget fit and timeline and books a consultation or site visit for anyone worth pursuing further.

The math behind the model

At this volume, Combine Design doesn't need a high close rate to hit ₹1CR/M — it needs a consistent one. A large lead pool means the studio's revenue isn't dependent on any single lead converting; it's dependent on the sales team working the pipeline every day and closing a steady percentage of a very large number. That's the core logic of a volume model: even a modest, repeatable close rate compounds into serious revenue when there are 400 conversations happening every month instead of 40.

What changed for Combine Design

Before this system, lead flow was inconsistent month to month, heavily reliant on referrals and builder relationships that the studio didn't fully control. After launch, lead flow became a predictable, scalable input — something the studio could dial up or down deliberately rather than something that happened to them. The sales team went from having gaps in their day to having a full calendar of calls, every single day, across both cities.

The result

A predictable, scalable stream of roughly 400 leads a month across Bangalore and Hyderabad that keeps a full sales team consistently busy, converts at a steady rate, and lands at ₹1CR/M in revenue month over month — without the studio depending on referrals or builder contacts to hit the number.

Why this model fits Combine Design specifically

This exact setup wouldn't be the right call for every studio. It works for Combine Design because the sales infrastructure to handle high call volume already existed, the two markets they operate in are large enough to sustain that volume without saturating, and the studio's economics work at a broader range of ticket sizes — so a bigger, more varied lead pool is a strength rather than a liability. A studio without that sales capacity, or one that only wants to take on a handful of large, premium projects, would be a better fit for the filtration model used with Pentagram Expert below.

Case Study 02 — Filtration

The situation

Pentagram Expert came in wanting the opposite of a high-volume pipeline. NCR is a market where the highest-value interior design projects rarely come from someone who clicked an ad five minutes ago — they come from clients who've already formed an opinion about the studio's taste, credibility, and quality before they ever pick up the phone. Pentagram Expert's ambition was to work on fewer, larger, better projects, not to run a call centre. Feeding that goal with a high-volume, low-friction funnel would have been actively counterproductive: it would have flooded a lean sales process with unqualified conversations that ate time without ever being close to a fit.

On top of the funnel problem, the brand itself wasn't doing any selling. The Instagram presence was inconsistent and didn't reflect the actual quality of the studio's work, which meant that even genuinely interested prospects who checked the profile before enquiring weren't getting convinced by it.

Why filtration was the right call here

Two things pointed away from a volume model for Pentagram Expert. First, the studio's target project size meant every won client represented a large share of monthly revenue — there was no need for hundreds of conversations when a handful of the right ones could hit the number. Second, the sales process here is inherently longer and more consultative: higher-ticket interior design decisions involve more stakeholders, more back-and-forth, and more trust-building than a quick phone qualification call. A lean sales process built for depth, not throughput, needed a funnel that did the filtering before the phone ever rang — not after.

The approach: filtration over volume

  • Website-first funnel instead of Instant Forms. Ads route to a proper landing page rather than a native lead form. That extra step — leaving the ad platform, loading a page, actually reading before converting — naturally filters out the low-intent clicks that Instant Forms would have let straight through. Only people willing to engage with the brand a little further end up converting, which is exactly the point.
  • Heavier pre-qualification built into the funnel itself. Budget ranges, project scope, and timeline questions are surfaced before a lead is treated as sales-ready, rather than discovered for the first time on the sales call. This meant the sales team stopped spending time re-qualifying people who were never going to be a fit.
  • A full brand and Instagram overhaul run alongside the ad strategy. Feed, highlights, and profile presentation were rebuilt to consistently reflect the actual quality of Pentagram Expert's completed projects. This mattered for two reasons: paid traffic now lands on a website and social presence that reads as premium and credible instead of unfinished, and organic followers who were already following the studio started converting into enquiries on their own, without any ad spend behind them.
  • Selective, narrow targeting. Rather than targeting broadly across NCR, campaigns were narrowed to the specific micro-markets and household budget bands that actually match the studio's real project size — deliberately leaving reach and impressions on the table in exchange for a tighter match between who sees the ad and who can actually afford the work.
  • A sales process built for depth, not speed. Because the funnel had already done the filtering, the sales team's job shifted from "qualify fast" to "close well" — fewer calls, but each one longer, more consultative, and far more likely to end in a signed project.

Campaign structure & ad split

The account is deliberately smaller and more concentrated than a volume account, split across three campaign types:

  • Conversion campaigns to the website (roughly 55–60% of budget). Objective set to on-site conversions (form completion), not leads or traffic, so the algorithm optimises toward people who actually finish the qualification form rather than just click through.
  • Retargeting campaigns (roughly 25% of budget). Served to website visitors who didn't complete the form, Instagram profile visitors, and engaged video viewers — this is where a large share of the eventual 50 qualified leads actually convert, on their second or third touch rather than their first.
  • Brand & reach campaigns (roughly 15–20% of budget). Run separately from the direct-response campaigns with the explicit goal of growing Instagram engagement and profile visits in the target localities, feeding the organic side of the funnel rather than chasing an immediate form-fill.

Unlike the Combine Design account, there's no multi-city split to manage — the entire budget is concentrated inside a small number of premium NCR localities, which keeps the audience narrow enough that filtration can happen through targeting and not just through the form.

Ad formats & creative mix

  • Cinematic project walkthroughs — longer-form, higher-production video shot to feel editorial rather than promotional, built to do the trust-building that a quick reel can't.
  • Long-form before/after reels that show the full scope and craftsmanship of a project rather than a quick transformation cut, aimed at signalling quality over speed.
  • Client testimonial videos filmed on-site in completed homes, used mainly in retargeting to close the loop with people who've already seen the brand once.
  • Creative volume here is intentionally low compared to the Combine Design account — fewer ads, each held to a much higher production bar, since the goal is one strong first impression rather than testing dozens of cheap variants.

The funnel, step by step

  1. Ad served to a narrow, locality- and income-signal-targeted audience across Instagram and Facebook feed and Reels.
  2. Landing page, not an Instant Form — the click takes the user to a dedicated page built around portfolio proof, testimonials, and brand credibility before any form appears.
  3. Qualification form on the page asks for budget range, project type, timeline, and locality — framed as a fit-check rather than a generic contact form, which itself discourages window-shoppers from finishing it.
  4. Submissions are screened against fit criteria before being treated as sales-ready; genuine fits are routed straight to a booked consultation slot, while borderline submissions are held for a follow-up rather than an immediate hard sales push.
  5. Consultation call is long-format and consultative, closer to a design discussion than a sales pitch — appropriate for a decision this size, and only possible because the funnel already filtered out the leads that weren't worth that time investment.

The math behind the model

At 50 leads a month, Pentagram Expert doesn't need volume to compound — it needs conversion quality to compound. Because the funnel filters out budget mismatches and low-intent traffic before a lead is counted at all, the leads that do land are disproportionately likely to already be a fit on budget, scope, and taste. That shift — from "many leads, low close rate" to "few leads, high close rate" — is what lets 50 enquiries a month produce the same ₹1CR/M that Combine Design generates from 400.

What changed for Pentagram Expert

Before this system, enquiries were inconsistent and largely dependent on the studio's existing network, and the brand's online presence undersold the actual quality of its work. After the rebuild, the studio had a repeatable paid channel producing a small, steady stream of well-matched enquiries every month, a website and Instagram presence that actively builds trust before a sales conversation even starts, and a sales calendar built around fewer, longer, higher-stakes conversations instead of a high volume of short qualification calls.

The result

A lean, high-quality pipeline of around 50 leads a month, filtered hard enough at the top of the funnel that a much higher share convert into signed projects — landing at ₹1CR/M in revenue with a fraction of the leads Combine Design needs, plus a brand and Instagram presence that keeps compounding organically outside of paid spend.

Why this model fits Pentagram Expert specifically

This approach works for Pentagram Expert because the studio's ambition was explicitly fewer, bigger, better projects rather than maximum throughput, and because its sales process is built for depth rather than speed. A studio without a distinct brand story to lean on, or one with a large sales team built for high call volume, would get far more value from the Combine Design volume model above — filtration only pays off when there's a real brand and a real sales process built to make the most of fewer, better conversations.
